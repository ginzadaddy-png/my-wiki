### Intro

**0:00** · No matter where you go on planet earth, there is one shared cultural obsession that you’re almost guaranteed to find, and that, is the sword. Europe has Excalibur belonging to king arthur, the islamic world has Zulfiqar belonging to the legendary caliph Ali and in japan they’ve got Kusinagi, one of the three great imperial treasures, basically every culture in the world from the Aztecs to the chinese has a long cultural fascination with swordplay. Which is weird, because from a military perspective, swords have never really been that important.

**0:32** · Swords require a lot of resources to make, need years of training to be used effectively and are only really useful in one on one fights or from horseback, not as part of a melee. In actuality, spears and bows have been the defacto weapon of choice for most human armies, that was, obviously, until the gun came along. The only real exception is the roman gladius, which could only be afforded when the empire was at its peak, and was exclusively used for stabbing, not slashing or fencing like most swords, which technically makes it more of a short spear but let’s not split hairs here.

**1:02** · So, why is our cultural lexicon filled with stories about legendary heroes wielding equally legendary swords, historically inaccurate battles featuring a lot of swords and space wizards with their things that may as well be swords if they rarely got used in a fight?

**1:19** · Well, the very things that make swords ineffective as a military weapon are exactly why they became such powerful symbols. Owing to the prestige of being able to own and use one effectively, swords have been the historical weapon of choice for aristocrats, elite soldiers and mercenaries - the kinds of people who get remembered in artworks, poetry and stories - meaning that over time the sword has become synonymous with skill in battle, epic duels between masters of the weapon and people with the power to lead armies, usually with a symbol of their status at their side, cementing the sword’s place in history and in our cultural vocabulary.

**1:52** · So, what does ANY of that have to do with videogames? Well, videogames are similarly obsessed with swordplay, from ninja gaiden, to the legend of zelda to the very best soulslikes, chances are if a game has a legendary hero, a master of the martial arts or a deadly villain, they’re gonna fight with a sword, even when it doesn’t really make sense, because swords are cool and people who use them are important. I think the key to translating the high drama and tension of all good sword fights into videogame form lies in this feeling of you and your actions being important, where destiny hangs in the balance, and a single move holds the difference between victory and defeat.

**2:30** · When a game doesn’t make you feel powerful and like your actions don’t matter, it’s irrelevant how well thought out the level design is or how balanced the combat systems are, it’ll never be able to replicate the cinematic joy of a good old fashioned sword fight.

**2:43** · Take an elder scrolls game, for example - even a regular bandit coming at you with a sharp stick can easily withstand multiple direct hits from your weapon, barely even flinching at being stabbed in the chest and doing similarly negligible damage to you in return, so you just sort of stand there spamming the attack button until he falls over.

**3:00** · That’s not to say that combat in the elder scrolls games isn’t basically pretty good, but there’s a reason why people gravitate to the ubiquitous stealth archer build over traditional hacky slashy gameplay, and that’s because oneshotting people from stealth with ridiculous multipliers sells the fantasy of being a sneaky assassin, wheras playing foam-padded safety combat doesn’t do a good job of making you feel like a master swordsman.

### The Basics

**3:21** · So how can games be both enough of a powerfantasy to make you feel cool, whilst also creating a enough of a sense of tension to make your actions feel meaningful?

**3:29** · Well, it’s best to start with the absolute basics, namely the vocabulary of offensive and defensive moves that you’re going to be using in any given fight.

**3:37** · If we look at any good cinematic swordfight, the combatants aren’t just wailing on one another, they’re parrying and dodging eachother’s attacks, using terrain to their advantage and even managing to inject a little personal flair into their combat style.. By encouraging players to think more laterally during a sword duel, and getting them to naturally incorporate a wide repertoire of moves, each of those small improvisational and reactive decisions become more meaningful.

**4:00** · Compare Jedi Fallen order to Sekiro, two games with very similar combat styles, but vastly different levels of success when it comes to execution. In Sekiro you have a huge variety of moves at your disposal, each with their own unique place in combat, you’ve got a regular attack, a thrust, a block, a parry, a dodge, a dodge attack, jumps, special counter moves, and I haven’t even mentioned your prosthetic tools like the shuriken which can interrupt enemies mid combo or the big axe which is just cool.

**4:26** · Mastering the game is less about frame-perfect reaction times and more about knowing which parts of your toolset to use against which enemy, creating these great moments as you duke it out with bosses, figuring out their weaknesses, how to counter their attacks, until you’re effortlessly dodging, parrying and dancing around their moves like a master.

**4:45** · Jedi fallen order tries many of the same things, it also has a variety of dodges counters and force moves for you to use, a lot of which feel really fun to incorporate into your fighting.

**4:55** · Unfortunately, they’re pretty much all trumped by an ancient and deadly sith technique that the \*\*jedi\*\* would never teach you, and it’s just sort of hammering the X button until all the storm troopers fall over. Cal’s force powers are fairly underwhelming and his parry and dodge feel sluggish, so whilst they are useful and have their place in combat, it’s never really worth using them over just spamming out more attacks, meaning you never get the fun feeling of mastering a toolset and you approach basically every fight the same way, like a bloodthirsty murder robot.

**5:24** · Of course, even when executed well, complex toolsets can have their downsides, the sheer amount of stuff to get your head around can present a pretty steep learning curve and unless handled carefully, they can often lead to binary “do this to win” mechanics, like sekiro’s anti-animal weapon or its borderline mandatory mikiri counter for spear-based enemies. Instead, many more approachable games give players only a few options but give them a lot more flexibility.

**5:51** · For example, for Honor uses some relatively simplistic controls to simulate blocking, feints and the need to break your opponent’s guard purely through a few button presses and directional inputs, giving fights a brilliant kinetic feel - particularly when combined with each fighter’s special abilities, that create a fast paced and nicely arcadey style of play whilst still remaining nice and intuitive for more causal players.

### Combat

**6:14** · However, simple control schemes don’t necessarily mean less nuanced gameplay, in fact some games can only have a few core moves, but still manage to create an incredible level of depth from them.

**6:25** · Metal Gear Rising: Revengance, the greatest named videogame of all time does just that. Your toolset is fairly basic, consisting of light and heavy attacks, a parry and a special slowmo move called blade time, but it’s in how you fit these things together that the true depth of the game shines.

**6:41** · Light attacks are better for staggering enemies and breaking through their defences, whilst heavy attacks lay down the hurt, and the brilliant blade time mechanic lets you tactically destroy enemy weapons, cut apart their armour and even chop off limbs to shift the battle in your favour. It’s a testament to just how well this simple series of moves organically fit together and flow into graceful but deadly combat that despite beating rising three times, I could not tell you a single combo in that game, my understanding is entirely intuitive and the game is better for it.

**7:05** · In fact, MG RR’s combat is so elegant in its design that its best and most memorable fights are the ones with the fewest gimmicks. Your duel against jetstream sam in the desert is a no frills one on one that purely tests your fundamentals as you duke it out, ducking and weaving around eachother in a blade ballet, all over who gets to take home robopuppy which is his canon name don’t try and correct me.

**7:31** · However, a perception of skill isn’t the only factor when it comes to fun of swordplay, you also need to feel powerful and like you’re up against equally deadly foes.

**7:41** · When your strikes feel weak and your enemies don’t pose a threat, then it’s hard to feel cool, no matter how well you’ve mastered combat. All of Dark Souls’ worst bosses aren’t the ones that are easy or unitutive, they’re the ones that boil down to a single boring formula, giant, unthreatening damage sponges that take ages to kill, and ruin the feeling of triumphing over incredible odds. The trio of big boys in dark souls 1? Hug their bums and stab them over and over. The coveteous demon in Dark souls 2? Hug his bum and stab him over and over?

**8:11** · The curse rotted greatwood in Dark souls 3? Hug his crotch and stab its weird eggs. These fights are battles of attrition more so than anything else, and a five minute grind against a boss you’ve almost got to let kill you almost makes you feel weaker than a pitched battle against a much more dangerous foe that’s also much more fragile like the capra demon, persuer or abyss watchers.

**8:32** · By dialing up the relative power of each attack, and making both players and enemies much more fragile games can simultaneously make swordfights much more exhilarating whilst also boosting their tactical depth.

### Tactics

**8:43** · In Bushido Blade for the PS1, each attack does so much damage that fights are often decided by a single blow, even a glancing hit to someone’s leg can cripple them, massively reducing their ability to dodge and move around, and attacks that cleanly strike the head or torso killing instantly.

**8:58** · This creates awesomely tense fights that see you squaring off with your opponent only to meet them in a sort of dance as you’re forced to carefully balance offense and defence because one undefended attack from a skilled opponent who knows where to strike could mean game over.

**9:11** · Ghostrunner goes a step further, instead of just limiting health pools to make each fight nice and tense, it turns the feeling of lethality up to 11 by making all damage lethal, both to you and enemies. Every enemy in the game will die to a single swing of your sword, creating this fantastic feeling of power as you chop your way through unsuspecting foes, however, even the most basic enemy class - this guy with a pistol is a real and tangible threat because if you stop paying attention for one second, you’re dead.

**9:36** · This, combined with the game’s focus on lightning fast platforming encourages speedy hit and run tactics that see you taking advantage of your comparatively sluggish foes, weaving your way through fields of fire to create an opening before dashing in for the kill and springing back out.

**9:54** · Ghostrunner’s regular enemies aren’t particularly complex in terms of design, but when combined with the game’s knife’s edge design, they create some great moments of tension.

**10:01** · These fellow swordsmen are a personal favourite, they’re quite easy to kill, you just need to parry them and follow up, but this means that they’re very time consuming to deal with, forcing you to either dance around them and risk getting stabbed in the back, or trying to fight one whilst under fire from other enemies, forcing you to do pull off some risky deflections.

**10:14** · A swordfight is nothing without meaningful stakes, but creating a dramatic one isn’t just a matter of making death super punishing, as Ghostrunner’s bosses prove, having to perform flawless gameplay non stop can get exhausting, and getting booted back to the very start after just one mistake is incredibly demoralising. Many punishing games make the mistake of feeling satisfyingly lethal in the moment, but failing to keep that satisfaction going between failures.

**10:39** · As strange as it sounds, this problem of sustaining the tension of a fight can actually be solved by making them bigger and longer and separating them into multiple parts. In Nidhogg, a low-fi fencing game, combat is for the most part one hit kills, and that makes fun moves like throwing your sword or going for kicks incredibly exhilarating, but regardless of the outcome of a single skirmish, the battle isn’t over, as a game of Nidhogg is only won when a player manages to get all the way to their opponent’s side of the map.

**11:14** · By positioning Nidhogg’s battles as a series of loosely connected skirmishes, the game creates this brilliant tug of war element where you’re encouraged to play more aggressively and take more risks because you’re always going to respawn a few seconds later. Because each game takes place over multiple sequential battles, and not a single clash, there’s room for players to use the momentum of a running battle to gain the upper hand, use mind games and fakeouts as well as epic comebacks - there is nothing quite like a long nidhogg game that finally reaches a climactic confrontation at the FINAL SCREEEEN only for the would-be winner to be pushed back right at the final moment, it’s glorious.

**11:50** · Of course, this practice of staggering failure states can also be extended into single player games, by breaking up fights into multiple distinct parts, they can escape the demoralizing consequences of binary success or failure design and instead embrace the push and pull of an authentic swordfight against an opponent who’s more than a series of pre-scripted moves on a timer.

**12:07** · Furi is the absolute king of this design, each of its bosses have multiple phases, and with each health bar you whittle down, their attack patterns and mood change - like The Hand who starts off as a really defensive warrior focused on blocking and responding to your attacks, but as you damage him more and more, he gets increasingly aggressive, abandoning his cool shield in phase four and in phase five coming at you with this gigantic last-ditch mega combo you need to parry your way through.

### Design

**12:37** · Similarly, you have three little health pips of your own, meaning that bosses can reduce your health to zero, and all that happens is that you lose a life and their health gets reset. This reincorporation of an oldschool bit of design means that it’s fine if the bosses are difficult or start throwing out some unfair surprises, because even if you get taken completely off guard, you’ll likely have a safety net to fall back on and you’ll only lose one phase worth of progress to begin with. In addition, parrying a boss’ melee attacks will restore some health, which is a fantastic way of encouraging players who are near death to stay in the fight and risk it all to try and regain some ground, rather than having them resort to cheesy, defensive strategies just to say alive.

**13:14** · To go back to sekiro, it also utilises this technique to make you feel more powerful, as well as make bosses feel like the climactic engagements they ought to be.

**13:23** · In sekiro, regular enemies can be dispatched incredibly quickly, often in a single blow, giving you that great feeling of being a lethal sneaky shinobi, but bossfights, usually taking the form of a fellow master of the martial arts are much more involved.

**13:37** · Simply reducing a bosses’ health to zero or completely staggering them once isn’t enough to end the fight, instead, you’ve got to deathblow them twice or sometimes even three times to kill them for good. And with each inaccurately named deathblow you perform, they get faster, and learn more tricks, like lady butterfly who starts summoning minions, or Genichiro Ashina who gets cool lightning powers. On the flipside, you also get the power to resurrect, which has the dual effect of reducing the unfairness factor that soulslike games are somewhat legendary for whilst also putting you on an equal playing field with the bosses, and ultimately, isn’t that kind of the point?

**14:09** · To feel just as powerful as these larger than life figures from fiction, but with the physicality and stakes that come from a grounding in something approaching reality.

**14:18** · Whether you’re giving players a suite of cool abilities that make them feel like a master of the blade, or putting them up against foes just as deadly as they are, you can make the argument the games I’ve talked about in no way replicate real swordfighting, but I think that’s kind of the point. Our infatuation with hacking and slashing heroes or mythical blades dished out by women in ponds doesn’t come from historical facts but the legends and myths that have built up around them over time.

**14:42** · The fun of swordplay in my mind doesn’t come from brutal wars where you die instantly in a medieval meatgrinder, though they can be fun too, but in the opportunity to put myself in the shoes of those same heroes and feel for a moment like I’m a part of a larger than life epic all of my own. Maybe in a thousand years some wise scholar will dig up a copy of dark souls and muse “hmm yess indeed those archers in annor londo truly are bullshit - hmm yah, what WAS the deal with those archers in Anor Londo? What were those dumbdumbs in the dark ages thinking? Maybe we were never meant to know… cool swords though.
